# Awaab's Law — Hazards in Social Housing (Phase 1)

> **Primary source:** [gov.uk/government/publications/awaabs-law-guidance-for-social-landlords/awaabs-law-guidance-for-social-landlords-timeframes-for-repairs-in-the-social-rented-sector](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/awaabs-law-guidance-for-social-landlords/awaabs-law-guidance-for-social-landlords-timeframes-for-repairs-in-the-social-rented-sector)
> **Statutory basis:** Social Housing (Regulation) Act 2023, s.42 → inserted s.10A into the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985
> **Regulations:** Hazards in Social Housing (Prescribed Requirements) (England) Regulations 2025
> **Phase 1 commenced:** 27 October 2025
> **Last verified:** 2026-04-26

## What it is

Awaab's Law was named after Awaab Ishak, a 2-year-old who died in December 2020 from prolonged exposure to mould in a social rented home in Rochdale. The law makes it a **legal obligation** for social landlords to investigate and fix specific hazards within fixed statutory timeframes. Failure exposes the landlord to enforcement action by the Regulator of Social Housing and to private legal action by tenants.

## Who it covers

Verbatim from the gov.uk guidance:

> "almost all social housing occupied under a tenancy"

Specifically, properties **let by registered providers of social housing** (housing associations, ALMOs, and local authority landlords). It does **not** apply to:

- Private rented sector (yet — the Decent Homes Standard extension via RRA 2025 covers PRS instead)
- Long leaseholds (where the leaseholder is effectively the owner)
- Owner-occupied homes

## Phase 1 scope (live from 27 October 2025)

Phase 1 covers:

> "all emergency hazards" and "all damp and mould hazards that present a significant risk of harm to tenants"

## Phase 1 — exact timeframes

> Source: gov.uk Awaab's Law guidance, last updated 27 October 2025

### Emergency hazards

| Step | Timeframe |
|---|---|
| Investigate the hazard and complete safety work to make safe | **Within 24 hours** of the landlord becoming aware |
| Provide a written summary of findings to the tenant | **Within 3 working days** of the investigation concluding |

**Definition of an "emergency hazard"** (verbatim from the guidance):

> "a risk that a reasonable lessor with the relevant knowledge would take steps to make safe within 24 hours, including gas leaks, broken boilers, exposed wiring, and prevalent mould affecting tenant health."

### Significant damp and mould hazards

| Step | Timeframe |
|---|---|
| Begin the investigation | **Within 10 working days** of the landlord becoming aware |
| Provide a written summary of findings to the tenant | **Within 3 working days** of the investigation concluding |
| Complete safety work | **Within 5 working days** after the investigation concludes |
| Begin preventative work | **Within 5 working days** of the investigation concluding (or within **12 weeks** if practical preparation is required) |

**Definition of a "significant hazard"** (verbatim):

> "a risk that a reasonable lessor with the relevant knowledge would take steps to make safe as a matter of urgency."

## Phased implementation timetable

| Phase | Effective date | Hazards covered |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1** | 27 October 2025 | Emergency hazards + significant damp/mould |
| **Phase 2** | October 2026 | Excess cold/heat, falls, structural collapse, fire, electrical, hygiene hazards |
| **Phase 3** | October 2027 | All remaining HHSRS hazards (except overcrowding) |

## How "becoming aware" is defined

The clock starts when the landlord becomes aware of a hazard through any reasonable means — tenant report, complaint, repairs visit, surveyor inspection, or third-party referral (council, doctor, social worker). It is not limited to formal complaints in writing.

## What "make safe" means in 24 hours

The 24-hour duty is to make the property **safe** — it does **not** require the permanent repair to be completed in 24 hours. Examples:

- Gas leak → isolate gas supply, vent the property, secure the appliance (permanent fix may need a Gas Safe engineer site-visit)
- Exposed wiring → isolate the circuit and physically cover or remove access (permanent re-wire may follow)
- Active leak from upstairs → stop the water at source and protect possessions (permanent plumbing fix may follow)
- Severe mould affecting health → temporary decant if needed, urgent fungicidal treatment, identify root cause for follow-up

## Reporting and record-keeping

Landlords must keep records of:
- The date the hazard was reported / identified
- The investigation findings
- The written summary provided to the tenant (and the date it was sent)
- The work completed and dates

These records will be material in any enforcement action by the Regulator of Social Housing or in tenant litigation under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985.

## What QB2 should never do

- **Never** tell a social landlord they have "a few weeks" to investigate damp/mould. The clock is **10 working days** to start, **5 working days** to act after findings.
- **Never** treat a Phase-1 hazard report as a routine repair. It carries legal force.
- **Never** suggest the landlord cancel the appointment because the tenant doesn't want them in. Statutory access provisions in the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 / Housing Act 1985 apply, and refusal does not freeze the clock indefinitely — landlord must document attempts.
