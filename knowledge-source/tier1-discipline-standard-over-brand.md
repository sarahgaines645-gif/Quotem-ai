# Discipline — Specify by Standard, Not by Brand

> **Rule:** A UK construction or property specification anchors on the harmonised BS / BS EN / PAS standard, not a manufacturer brand. PAS 24 not "Yale". BS EN 1057 not "Wednesbury". BS 5839-6 not "Aico".
> **Audience:** specifiers, surveyors, contractors, building-control officers, council procurement teams, landlords reviewing tenders, legal/compliance reviewers.
> **Last verified:** 2026-04-26

## Why

Public-sector procurement law requires **non-discriminatory specification**. The Procurement Act 2023 (in force 24 February 2025) carries forward the principle that contracting authorities must not specify in a way that favours a particular supplier without justification.
Source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/54/contents

Naming a brand without "or equivalent" excludes alternatives and breaches that principle. The standard captures the performance; multiple manufacturers can meet it. This applies whether the specifier is a council, housing association, surveyor producing a schedule of works, or a private landlord drafting a tender.

## UKCA / CE marking

Construction products placed on the GB market must carry the **UKCA** mark (or **CE** during the recognised transition) where covered by a designated standard, accompanied by a Declaration of Performance (DoP). The standard cited on the DoP is the load-bearing reference — not the brand name.
Source: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/using-the-ukca-marking
Source (Construction Products Regulations 2013): https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/1387/contents/made

## How to read a BS / BS EN code

`BS EN 1935:2002` =

- **BS** — British Standard
- **EN** — European Norm (harmonised across CEN members)
- **1935** — the document number (here, single-axis hinges)
- **:2002** — year of publication of that edition

A grade or class often follows in product literature, e.g. `BS EN 1935 Grade 13` (severe-duty hinge — 200,000 cycles, doors up to 120 kg, suitable for FD30/FD60 fire doors). A `BS XXXX-N` form (e.g. `BS 5839-6`) means **Part N** of a multi-part standard (here, Part 6 — fire detection in dwellings).

## How specifications are typically written

A correctly drafted specification states performance + standard, optionally followed by an example of compliance:

- "PVCu rainwater goods to BS EN 607 / BS EN 12200 / BS EN 1462"
- "Doorsets to PAS 24:2022 with multi-point lock and 3-star anti-snap cylinder to TS 007"
- "Fire detection: BS 5839-6 Grade D1, Category LD2"

Brand names appear only as "examples of compliance" or in tendered alternatives. This pattern is used across NHF model documents, council schedules of works, surveyor specifications and architect's drawings packages.
Source: National Housing Federation — https://www.housing.org.uk/

## How to verify a product claim

1. Find the **Declaration of Performance** (DoP) or technical data sheet.
2. Confirm the **standard cited** matches what the spec asks for.
3. Confirm any **grade / class** (e.g. PAS 24, Grade 13, Class A2-s1,d0) is on the document — not just the standard number.
4. For fire / safety / structural items, confirm a **third-party certification body** (UKAS-accredited) is named.

## Common errors and red flags

- A spec written as "Yale lock" / "Aico alarm" / "Velux window" with no underlying standard. The standard is the spec; the brand is an example.
- A contractor or supplier claiming "it's the same as PAS 24" without a DoP that cites PAS 24. If it isn't on the DoP, it isn't certified.
- UKCA/CE absence on a safety-critical item treated as a minor admin gap. Without it, the product cannot legally be placed on the GB market for that use under the Construction Products Regulations 2013.
- A grade or class quoted in marketing copy but absent from the data sheet — the data sheet is what counts.
